About

A private museum of ordinary days.

Photos catch the highlights. Everything else evaporates — the meal you keep reordering, the walk you didn't think to remember. Curio keeps the texture.

The problem

Most people who try to keep a diary stop within a fortnight, and the reason is nearly always the same: the blank page. At the end of a long day, writing about it is one more task.

Meanwhile the tools that promise to remember for us ask for something uncomfortable in return — an account, a subscription, and a copy of your life on somebody else's servers. When one of those companies is bought, or shuts down, or changes its mind, the memories go with it. That has already happened to people, more than once.

Our mission

To help people keep the texture of their ordinary lives — without asking them to write it, and without asking them to hand it over.

Two promises, held together. Removing the effort is what makes a diary survive past January. Removing the server is what makes it survive past the company.

Our vision

A world where remembering your own life does not require an account.

Software that holds something as personal as a diary should answer to the person who wrote it and to nobody else. We would like that to be ordinary rather than remarkable — and we would like it to be true in Kiswahili and Naija Pidgin as much as in English, because the people underserved by software are rarely the ones it gets built for first.

What we believe

Your device is enough

Phones are extraordinary computers. A diary does not need a data centre; it needs to work on the train, in the air, and in ten years' time.

No server, no betrayal

The strongest privacy promise is not a policy. It is an application with nowhere to send anything.

Say the limits out loud

Curio tells you when a feature would send data elsewhere, and refuses to record a room that has not been told. Trust is built where it costs something.

Everyone's language

The placards are composed natively in fourteen languages and dialects — not machine-translated buttons over English prose.

Yours to leave with

Export as a plain file, readable text, or a printed book. Nothing is locked in, because lock-in is how memories get held hostage.

Free, and staying free

There are no servers to pay for, so there is no subscription to justify — and no advertising, because your diary is not an audience.

How it works, briefly

You keep small things — a photo, where you were, what you ate, who you were with, something you read, a meeting. Curio composes each into a museum placard, on the device, and finds the patterns you would never notice about your own life.

Everything is stored locally. A Recovery Kit — your whole archive sealed with a passphrase only you know — is what lets it survive a lost phone. Full technical detail is in the privacy page.
